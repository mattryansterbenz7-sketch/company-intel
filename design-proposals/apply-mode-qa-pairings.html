How matching works (semantic, not regex)

When a form question comes in, Claude reads it and routes by meaning. “What’s your target OTE?” · “Salary expectations incl. commission?” · “Total comp year one?” all land in Compensation — and Coop re-writes a fresh answer from the stored pairings to fit the exact phrasing being asked. Nothing robotic, nothing regex-bound.

Verbatim paste only if you mark a pairing “use literally.” Low-confidence questions surface the top 2 candidates inline so you pick. No bucket match → fall back to the profile.

Why 7 instead of 9

“Sponsorship” and “Location/work arrangement” frequently co-occur in one question (“Are you authorized to work in X and willing to relocate?”) — splitting them forces awkward routing. “Culture/motivation” and “Why this role/company” collapse into Motivation (how you talk about yourself) and Why this opportunity (how you talk about them). The talk-track boundary stays clean.

How the chip behaves

When Coop uses a pairing to draft the answer, a Compensation answer chip sits above the drafted message. Flat fill at 10% coral, 11px, no gradient — calm, readable, doesn’t steal the eye from the answer body.

Click the chip to expand the full pairing reference inline. The expanded state shows:

  • Question pattern matched — the specific pairing, not the bucket
  • Tone preference applied — what voice Coop leaned into
  • Classifier confidence — low confidence = a signal to Matt to add an example to the pairing
  • Edit pairing link — one-click jump to Settings if the draft missed

When no pairing matches, the chip switches to the neutral state: No saved answer · drafted from profile. Same visual footprint, monochrome gray. This is the moment Matt would most want to save an answer — see the Correction workflow below.

5 Why these collapses Original 9 proposed → collapsed 7. Rationale per merge.
Original buckets Collapsed to Why
SponsorshipLocation / work arrangement → Sponsorship & Location These co-occur in one compound question more often than not (“Authorized to work in X? Willing to relocate?”). Splitting them forces awkward routing when the question spans both.
Culture / motivation(partial merge) → Motivation “Culture fit” questions are really “what are you looking for” questions in disguise. One bucket, clearer talk track: how you talk about yourself.
Why this roleWhy this company → Why this opportunity Both require the same move: connect your story to their story. The answer needs company-specific context injected at draft time either way — the saved template is structurally identical.
Compensation = Compensation Kept. Distinct enough to warrant its own bucket — tone and numerics rarely cross over into other answer types.
Start date / availability = Availability Renamed, not merged. “Availability” is tighter shorthand for the same answers.
Experience = Experience Kept. Behavioral / proof-point answers that reference specific past roles. No clean merge candidate.
Other = Other Kept as catch-all. If “Other” starts growing past ~4 answers, that’s a signal a new bucket is emerging.
6 Open questions for Matt Low-confidence decisions where I want a read before wiring Phase B.
Q1
Is “Why this opportunity” right, or keep as two separate buckets?

I collapsed “Why this role” and “Why this company” because both require the same structural move — connect your story to their story. But the talk tracks can diverge (role = skills/scope fit, company = mission/stage fit). If you find yourself writing meaningfully different templates, we split them back out.

Q2
Tone preference — per-pairing, per-bucket, or global?

Currently rendered as per-pairing (max flexibility). But per-pairing may be overkill — you might want all Compensation answers Direct and all Motivation answers Measured, without setting it 6 times. Alternative: default at bucket level, override at pairing level when needed.

Q3
“No saved answer” chip — passive or prompting?

Today the chip just reads “No saved answer · drafted from profile” (passive). Alternative: append “Want to save one?” with a small button — a gentle nudge to build up the library after every unmatched draft. Risk: noisy. Upside: the library fills itself.

Q4
Classification mechanism Phase B

Regex + keyword match is cheaper and deterministic. Haiku classifier is smarter but spends tokens on every question. My instinct: regex-first for obvious matches (e.g. “salary” → Compensation), Haiku fallback for ambiguous. Flagging here so Phase B scopes it — not a Phase A blocker.
